# "Real" Wars: Esthetics and Professionalism in Computer Animation

## Summary

Manovich applies Pierre Bourdieu's sociology of taste (from *Distinction*) to the field of 3D computer animation in the early 1990s. He argues that the aesthetic standards of computer animation — [[Smoothness]], [[Complexity]], and the deliberate "[[Computer Look]]" — are not neutral markers of technical progress toward realism but function as **mechanisms of professional distinction** that separate professionals from amateurs and legitimate the status (and pricing) of design firms.

## Key Arguments

- **Smoothness** as gatekeeping: Antialiasing, available only on expensive hardware, produces the "clean" look that separates professionals from amateurs. Even when natural irregularity could be modeled, professionals avoid it because roughness might be mistaken for cheap equipment.
- **Complexity** as cost signal: More movement, more geometric detail, more shading computation = more labor = higher cost to client. Visual complexity functions as a direct sign of a client's wealth and a designer's skill.
- **Computer look** as branding: Paradoxically, animations are designed to *look* computer-generated (wide lenses, rollercoaster cameras, abstract chrome environments) even though photorealism is technically possible — because the point is to signal the prestige of using cutting-edge technology.
- Aesthetic standards function *like* technical standards (e.g., NTSC, 35mm gauge) but are **more effective** at legitimating professional status because they appear disinterested and universal rather than explicitly industry-imposed.

## Theoretical Framework

- [[Pierre Bourdieu]] — *Distinction*: aesthetic taste legitimates social hierarchy
- Professional-amateur distinction in photography and film industries as historical precedent
- Parallel to how Hollywood's 35mm standard and elaborate production values excluded outsiders
