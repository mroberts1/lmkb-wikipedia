# Navigable Space

## Summary

A long, wide-ranging essay (later a chapter in *The Language of New Media*) exploring navigable 3D space as a key cultural form of computer culture. Manovich surveys computer games, VRML, motion simulators, scientific visualization, and art projects to analyze how spatial navigation through virtual environments becomes a dominant mode of interacting with all kinds of data and cultural content.

## Key Arguments

- **Navigable space as cultural form**: Just as cinema's key cultural form was the moving image, computer culture's key form may be navigable 3D space — used for games, data visualization, architecture, social interaction, and more.
- **Spatialization of everything**: Computer culture maps all information onto spatial structures — from file systems to VRML worlds to data visualization.
- **Art-historical space categories applied**: Uses Riegl (haptic/optic), Wölfflin (linear/painterly), Panofsky (aggregate/systematic) to analyze virtual spaces — finding them more "aggregate" and "haptic" than "systematic" and "optic."
- **Flâneur and navigator**: The computer user as flâneur, navigating virtual spaces — but with key differences from Baudelaire's/Benjamin's original concept. De Certeau's distinction between strategies (totalizing, from above) and tactics (local, from within) applied to navigation modes.
- **Artistic alternatives**: Jeffrey Shaw's *Legible City*, Char Davies' *Osmose*, Waliczky's custom perspectives — each offers an alternative to the default cinematic/perspectival mode of virtual navigation.
- **Non-places**: Marc Augé's concept of "non-places" (airports, highways, supermarkets) applied to virtual spaces — generic, transitory, identity-less.
