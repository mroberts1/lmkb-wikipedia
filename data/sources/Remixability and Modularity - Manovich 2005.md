# Remixability and Modularity

## Summary

An essay analyzing the interconnected phenomena of remixability and modularity in contemporary culture. Manovich argues that remix culture operates on a continuum from professional to vernacular, that modularity has been the key principle of mass production since Ford, and that while culture has always involved remixing, digital technology has dramatically amplified this by modularizing culture at a structural level (pixels, layers, hypertext).

## Key Arguments

- **Collaborative remixability**: The dramatic increase in information pathways (SMS, RSS, blogs, P2P, tagging) stimulates people to draw from multiple sources, remix, and share. Reception is "just a temporary station on information's path."
- **Professional and vernacular remix on same continuum**: Software makes remix easy for both professionals and amateurs; Internet makes locating and reusing material from any period trivial.
- **Multitrack mixing as paradigm**: Just as multitrack mixers (1972, Tom Moulton) made musical remixing standard, digital tools modularize all media for remixing.
- **Post-computer modularity without vocabulary reduction**: Unlike pre-computer modularity (limited blocks → limited forms), post-computer modularity can use parametric design to ensure every reuse produces variation. "Unlimited diversity" rather than repetition.
- **"Information wants to be ASCII"**: Web 2.0 moves from intricately packaged Flash objects to "straight" information (RSS, blog entries, text files) that can travel and be remixed more easily. "Content wants to be granular."
- **"We have never been modular"**: While modularity has been the basis of industrial production, cultural production has never been fully modularized — content is still made on a one-by-one basis by human teams. Computerization modularizes culture at a structural level (pixels, layers, HTML).
