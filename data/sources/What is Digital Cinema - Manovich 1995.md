# What is Digital Cinema?

## Summary

One of Manovich's most influential essays. He argues that digital cinema is not a radical break from cinema's past but a **return to its pre-cinematic origins** in animation and manual image construction. The privileged role of photographic live-action footage in 20th-century cinema was a historical exception; digital tools restore cinema's identity as a branch of painting and animation.

## Key Arguments

- **Cinema as the art of the index**: 20th-century cinema theory (Bazin, Barthes, Metz) defined cinema through its indexical relationship to reality — the camera records what is physically in front of it. Digital manipulation undermines this indexicality.
- **From animation to cinema**: Pre-cinematic devices (Zoetrope, Phenakistiscope) used hand-drawn images. Cinema suppressed this manual, artisanal origin by privileging photographic recording. Digital tools reverse this: "Cinema can no longer be clearly distinguished from animation."
- **What is digital cinema?**: Rather than live-action recording supplemented by special effects, digital cinema = "a particular case of animation that uses live-action footage as one of its many elements." Live action becomes raw material for compositing, painting, and animation.
- **The loop**: Pre-cinematic devices were based on short image loops. Cinema suppressed the loop. Digital media (GIFs, game cycles, interactive narratives) resurrects it as a fundamental narrative form.
- **From Kino-Eye to Kino-Brush**: Vertov's *Man with a Movie Camera* (1929) is reframed as the most important film of the 20th century — it anticipated digital cinema's key operations: compositing multiple realities, superimposing temporal layers, and revealing the constructed nature of the image.
- **Multimedia as "primitive" digital cinema**: Early multimedia (HyperCard, CD-ROMs) resembles the "primitive" period of cinema before classical conventions were established.
