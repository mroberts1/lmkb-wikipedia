# Scale Effects

## Summary

A lecture outline proposing **scaling** as an overarching concept for understanding the cultural effects of computerization. Manovich argues that computers radically scale up existing cultural and social forms, leading to new qualitative effects. He provides examples across speed, size, and media redefinition, and introduces the concept of "art after compression."

## Key Arguments

- **Scale as key concept**: We are used to thinking about effects of *new* technologies but not about effects of *scaling up* existing ones. McLuhan's key insight: "the 'message' of any medium or technology is the change of scale or pace or pattern that it introduces."
- **Speed**: Perspective calculation took human draftsmen hours; computers do it 80 times per second. This speed difference enabled new media (3D animation, virtual spaces).
- **Size**: Wall-sized displays (100 megapixels) and gigapixel images function in fundamentally new ways. "You can zoom into details while still maintaining the sense of the whole."
- **4K digital video as new medium**: Normal video flattens the world, making it prosaic. 4K video creates the opposite effect — "even most prosaic objects acquire a precious quality." Comparison to Dutch 17th-century painting (Svetlana Alpers's *The Art of Describing*).
- **From "new media" to "more media"**: The concept of scale should be central to media theory. We no longer deal with "old media" or "new media" — we deal with "more media."
- **Art after compression**: Human art has been a form of compression — condensing experience into symbols. Digital storage eliminates compression constraints. What kind of art can we create when we can capture the world without limitations?
