# The Mapping of Space: Perspective, Radar, and 3-D Computer Graphics

## Summary

Manovich traces the history of **visual nominalism** — the use of vision to capture the identity of individual objects and spaces by recording distances and shapes — from Renaissance perspective through radar to interactive 3D computer graphics. He argues that perspective is not merely an artistic convention but a practical instrument for measuring and controlling reality, and that its automation by computers represents the culmination of a process that began with Alberti.

## Key Arguments

- **William Ivins** (not Panofsky) provides the key insight: perspective establishes a precise, reciprocal metrical relationship between objects and their representations — it is a tool for businessmen, scientists, and engineers, not just artists.
- **Bruno Latour** extends this: perspective enables the manipulation of reality at a distance through its representations. You can measure the sun on a photograph; you can reconstruct a Roman church in London from a drawing.
- **Radar** is the quintessential 20th-century technology of visual nominalism: it reduces vision to pure spatial coordinates (positions of objects), discarding color, texture, and shape. It extends vision across the entire electromagnetic spectrum and operates in real-time.
- **Lacan** clarifies that perspective is not limited to sight — it is about "the mapping of space, not sight." Radar, sonar, infrared imaging all operate on the perspectival principle of connecting points by straight lines.
- **3D computer graphics** completes the automation: Larry Roberts' perspective-generating algorithm (early 1960s) drew directly on 19th-century German textbooks on perspectival geometry. The algorithm automates what draftsmen did manually.
- **Interactive computer graphics** (from Sutherland's Sketchpad onward) represents a revolution beyond automation: the ability to change the virtual camera's viewpoint in real-time and to interactively build/modify 3D models.

## Key Connections

- Links the Gulf War's "logistics of perception" (Virilio) to the long history of perspectival technologies
- SAGE and Lincoln Laboratory at MIT as the birthplace of interactive computer graphics (a theme that recurs in [[Archeology of a Computer Screen - Manovich 1995]] and [[The Labor of Perception - Manovich 1995]])
- The Renaissance → photography → computer graphics lineage as progressive automation of perspective
