# Don't Call it Art: Ars Electronica 2003

## Summary

A critical review of the 2003 Ars Electronica festival (theme: CODE) that broadens into a systematic analysis of where "digital art" / "software art" fits within the cultural landscape. Manovich argues that digital art is neither contemporary art (too medium-focused), nor modernism (lacks larger political/spiritual commitments), nor design (design field isn't devoted to "basic research"), but is closest in logic to computer science.

## Key Arguments

- **Festival critique**: Ars Electronica narrowed "software art" to algorithmically generated media, ignoring the diverse practices visible at README, Transmediale, and elsewhere. Festival came "full circle" back to 1980s computer imaging.
- **Not contemporary art**: Since the 1960s, contemporary art is fundamentally conceptual — medium and technique are secondary. Software art is firmly focused on its medium, putting it at odds with contemporary art's logic.
- **Not modernism ver. 2**: Modernist artists were driven by larger political, social, and spiritual values. Today's digital artists are "proper formalists" centered on software as medium.
- **Closest to computer science**: Both digital artists and computer scientists working on "cultural" parts of CS are true formalists investigating new possibilities for representation and communication.
- **Discourse matters**: "If brilliant computer images are not supported by equally brilliant cultural ideas, their life span is very limited." Works either get forgotten or incorporated into mainstream software as elementary building blocks.
- **Complementary limitations**: Contemporary art is "too historical" (every statement references other statements in the field); digital art has "no memory of its own history."
- **"Abstraction Now" as model**: Vienna's Künstlerhaus show successfully inserted software art within larger fields of contemporary cultural production — what Ars Electronica failed to do.
