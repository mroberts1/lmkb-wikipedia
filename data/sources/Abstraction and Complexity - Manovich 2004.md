# Abstraction and Complexity

## Summary

A major essay arguing that contemporary software-driven abstraction follows the paradigm of **complexity** rather than the **reduction** that defined modernist abstraction. Manovich traces how modernist art paralleled reductionist science (physics, chemistry, experimental psychology) in seeking basic elements, while today's software abstraction echoes complexity science (chaos theory, emergence, artificial life) in embracing dynamic, non-linear, evolving systems.

## Key Arguments

- **After Effects and the figuration/abstraction opposition**: Computerization "strengthened" figuration (via 3D graphics) while "blurring" it (via software compositing that hybridizes media). The computer metamedium creates hybrid images combining traces of many media.
- **Modernist reduction**: Modernist abstraction (Mondrian, Kandinsky, Malevich) paralleled modern science's reductionism. Experimental psychologists split visual experience into elements; artists adopted their simple visual stimuli. "Abstraction was born in psychological laboratories before it ever reached the gallery walls."
- **Complexity paradigm**: Since the 1960s, new sciences (chaos, complex systems, emergence, artificial life) look at complex dynamic non-linear systems. "Orderly ensemble properties can and do arise in the absence of blueprints."
- **Software abstraction as complexity**: Contemporary software works typically begin with minimal elements that evolve into complex, constantly changing images — the opposite of Mondrian's reduction from tree to grid. "The patterns of lines suggest the inherent complexity of the world that is not reducible to some geometric phenotype."
- **Evidence from "Abstraction Now"**: Systematic analysis of works in this exhibition confirms the aesthetics of complexity — Golan Levin's organic lines, biological references, systems oscillating between order and chaos.
- **Direct and indirect borrowing**: Just as modernist abstraction connected to reductionist science both directly (artists copying from psychology) and indirectly (shared imagination), software abstraction connects to complexity science both ways.
