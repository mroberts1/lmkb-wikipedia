# What Comes After Remix?

## Summary

An essay surveying remix culture across news, user-generated content, music, fashion, design, art, and software mash-ups, then asking: what comes after remix? Manovich traces the evolution of remixing from Tom Moulton's "chaste" 1972 disco remixes to aggressive sampling, analyzes why "remix" is better than "appropriation" as a term, and notes that while remixing is officially accepted in music, in other media it's considered copyright violation.

## Key Arguments

- **Remix as epistemology**: On the web, remix has become intrinsic to news communication — RSS feeds, Google News (algorithmic remix), blogs (commentary as remix), user-generated content (sampling professional content). "Remix epistemology" = generating knowledge by selecting and combining from numerous sources.
- **UGC as remix**: Bloggers republishing with commentary, anime music video makers combining professional anime and music, personal blogs assembling material from multiple sources — all forms of remix.
- **Evolution of remixing**: From Moulton's "chaste" reweighting of soundtracks (1972) to Coldcut's aggressive contrasting of voices, techno sounds, and house rhythms (1987). Term gradually broadened from music to all media.
- **"Remixing" vs. "appropriation"**: Remixing implies systematic reworking; appropriation implies mere transfer of a sign from one sphere to another (as in Duchamp's urinal or Richard Prince's copies). Remixing is the better term.
- **Sampling vs. quoting**: Quoting inserts fragments into new text; remixing systematically rearranges the whole. Quoting is a precedent for sampling (industrialized montage/collage), not remixing.
- **Three differences from modernist collage**: (1) Musical samples arranged in loops; (2) Sound allows blending into coherent whole, not just "clash" (Barthes); (3) Electronic musicians expect their works to be remixed.
- **What comes after?**: "Will we eventually get tired of cultural objects made from samples which come from the already existing database of culture?" Possibly — cultural regimes that seem permanent can collapse quickly (Soviet Union, post-Cold War optimism).
