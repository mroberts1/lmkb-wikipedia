# Computer Simulation and the History of Illusion

## Summary

An expanded version of arguments from [[Paradoxes of Digital Photography - Manovich 1994]] and [[Zeuxis meets RealityEngine - Manovich 1996]], examining how computer-based illusionism differs from traditional visual illusionism. Manovich argues that synthetic images are not inferior representations of reality but "perfectly realistic representations of a different reality" — cyborg vision. He also analyzes the temporal oscillation between illusion and its suspension in interactive media as a structural feature of modern society, not a technological artifact.

## Key Arguments

- **Beyond visual illusionism**: Traditional theories (Gombrich, Bazin) only address visual fidelity. Computer-based illusionism operates on multiple dimensions: visual appearance, bodily engagement, multi-sensory input, and simulation of physical behavior, natural phenomena, and human psychology.
- **Synthetic images as hyperreal**: CG images are not inferior but "too perfect" — unlimited resolution, no depth-of-field, no grain, saturated colors, geometric precision. They represent "the vision of a cyborg or a computer," a future sight cleansed of noise.
- **Jurassic Park as Socialist Realism**: Just as Socialist Realism superimposed the perfect communist future onto imperfect present reality, *Jurassic Park* superimposes future cyborg vision (CG) onto the familiar vision of film. CG images must be systematically degraded (resolution reduced, grain added, edges softened) to blend with film footage. Dinosaurs mask the disturbing reference to our cyborg future by placing it safely in the past.
- **From identification to action**: New media turns viewers into users. Images become interfaces and instruments. "What kinds of actions can be performed via an image" now matters as much as visual fidelity for the reality effect.
- **Meta-realism**: Interactive media oscillates between illusion and its suspension (pages loading, LOD switching, QuickTime VR deconstructing itself). This is not a bug but a structural feature — echoing Brecht's alienation effect but functioning differently. The user "invests into illusion precisely because she is given control over it."
- **Cognitive multi-tasking**: The oscillation between perception and action in interactive media reflects the broader cognitive demands of modern computing — constantly switching between different mental sets and applications.
