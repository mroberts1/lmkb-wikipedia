# From DV Realism to a Universal Recording Machine

## Summary

Manovich historicizes two opposing digital cinema aesthetics — **special effects spectacle** (Méliès tradition) and **DV realism** (Lumière tradition) — arguing that neither is genuinely new. What is truly unprecedented is the computer's capacity for **complete recording**: massive-scale capture and automated indexing of human experience across time, visual space, and social relationships.

## Key Arguments

- **Two traditions historicized**: Digital special effects descend from Méliès; DV realism (Dogma 95, *Timecode*, *Blair Witch Project*) descends from Lumière and 1960s cinéma vérité. Both traditions accompanied cinema from its origin.
- **Lumières were not documentarists**: Even *Arrival of a Train* was carefully staged. DV realism films (*Celebration*, *Timecode*) similarly construct highly artificial narratives — they use real time but compress dramatic events unrealistically.
- **From sampling to complete recording**: The genuinely new capacity is the shift from compressing reality into essential scenes to recording vast, continuous chunks of experience. Digital storage + automated indexing makes this possible across three dimensions: temporal granularity (years of video), visual granularity (360° recording), and social granularity (*The Sims* as social simulator).
- **Surveillance aesthetics**: *Timecode* adopts the visual strategies of video surveillance (multiple cameras, single location, real time) for fictional narrative. Television's original meaning (seeing at a distance) survives in surveillance and webcams.
- **Computer as universal recording machine**: The filmmaker must become an interface designer. "The trick may be simply to pour all of reality on a hard drive — and then figure out what kind of interface the user needs." Godard's *History of Cinema* as proto-database cinema.
