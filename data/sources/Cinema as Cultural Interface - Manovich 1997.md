# Cinema as a Cultural Interface

## Summary

One of Manovich's most important and comprehensive essays, later absorbed into *The Language of New Media*. He argues that the language of cultural interfaces in the 1990s is shaped by three competing traditions: the **printed word**, **cinema**, and **general-purpose HCI** — with cinema increasingly dominating. As computer culture translates cinematic grammar into software and hardware, cinema is becoming the universal cultural interface.

## Key Arguments

### Three traditions shaping cultural interfaces
1. **Printed word**: The page (from codex to HTML), hyperlinking (which privileges metonymy and flattens hierarchy), scrolling (a return to the papyrus roll). Hyperlinking reflects postmodern suspicion of hierarchy but reduces rhetoric to a single figure.
2. **Cinema**: The mobile virtual camera (becoming an interface to all data types, not just 3D), rectangular framing (inherited from painting via Alberti), cinematic editing conventions. Even VR uses cinema's rectangular frame and mobile camera.
3. **HCI**: Overlapping windows (a synthesis of temporal montage and montage-within-a-shot), menus, direct manipulation. Originally developed for military/scientific/office use, now awkwardly applied to cultural data.

### Key claims
- **Cinema as universal interface**: "From one cultural language among others, cinema is becoming the cultural interface, a toolbox for all cultural communication, overtaking the printed word."
- **Automation of cinematic grammar**: Computer games encode cinematic conventions (point of view, camera angles, editing) into software and hardware (Nintendo 64's camera control buttons). Once implemented in low-level code, conventions become invisible defaults.
- **Window interface = cinema synthesis**: Overlapping windows combine temporal montage (switching between windows) with montage-within-a-shot (multiple windows visible simultaneously), solving the problem Vertov pushed to its limit with superimposition.
- **Cultural interfaces as hybrid language**: An "uneasy path" between the immersive experience of traditional cultural objects and the control granularity of HCI. The screen simultaneously functions as window (illusionistic space) and instrument panel (controls).
- **Spatialization of postmodernism**: Digital media literally accomplishes what postmodern theory described metaphorically — replacing sequential with random access, hierarchy with hypertext, temporal narrative with spatial navigation.

## Alternative projects discussed

- Art+Com's *Invisible Shape of Things Past*: cinematic records placed back into 3D model of Berlin
