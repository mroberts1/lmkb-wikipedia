# 10 Key Texts on New Media Art

## Summary

An annotated bibliography/essay lamenting the digital art field's absent long-term memory. Manovich selects 10 texts (Youngblood, Reichardt, Goodman, Kittler, Benedikt, Artintact, ISEA 94, Ars Electronica 95, Aarseth, Poschardt) and argues that the field lacks canonical critical texts. He critiques the Whitney's 2001 "Bitstreams" as reactionary compared to 1968-70 museum shows.

## Key Arguments

- **Short memory problem**: Digital art field keeps "reinventing the wheel" — no critical text has achieved the canonical status of Greenberg, Krauss, Bazin, or Mulvey in their respective fields.
- **1968-70 shows were more radical**: "Cybernetic Serendipity," "The Machine as Seen at the End of the Mechanical Age," "Software," and "Information" were more ambitious and conceptually interesting than the 2001 museum shows.
- **Whitney 2001 as reactionary**: New media was "neutralized, diluted, rendered harmless" — reduced to flat images on walls with no computers or interactive works, positioned within familiar art history narratives.
- **Theory over criticism**: The field's most important texts are theoretical works about techno-culture at large (Kittler, Hayles, Turkle, Virilio) rather than art criticism per se. "This is ultimately very healthy."
- **Kittler as most important media theorist after McLuhan**: His *Discourse Networks* uniquely combines critical theory (Lacan, Foucault) with ideas about communication technologies' effects on culture.
