# Archeology of a Computer Screen

## Summary

Manovich constructs a genealogy of the screen as a cultural form, tracing it from Renaissance painting through cinema to the computer display and VR. He identifies three historical screen types (classical, dynamic, real-time) and examines the paradoxical relationship between the screen and the body: the progressive mobilization of the image was accompanied by the progressive imprisonment of the viewer.

## Key Arguments

### Screen genealogy
- **Classical screen**: A flat, rectangular surface intended for frontal viewing that acts as a window into another space of different scale. Spans from Alberti's window to the modern computer display. Proportions have barely changed in five centuries.
- **Dynamic screen** (c. 1895): Retains all properties of the classical screen but displays images changing over time. Cinema, television, video. Demands the viewer's complete identification with the screen image.
- **Real-time screen**: Radar, then television and computer monitors. The image updates continuously to reflect changes in the referent. "The image, in a traditional sense, no longer exists" — it is produced by sequential scanning, with different parts corresponding to different moments in time.
- **Interactive screen**: The computer screen (from SAGE onward) — the real-time screen becomes a means not only to display information but to give commands. The screen becomes a vehicle for affecting reality.

### Screen and the body
- **Progressive imprisonment**: Alberti's perspectival window → camera obscura (literal dark chamber) → photography (iron clamps for sitters) → cinema (collective prison of immobile spectators in the dark). The more mobile the image, the more immobile the viewer.
- **Barthes** on the screen as the "Organon of Representation" — the cut-out rectangle that defines all "dioptric arts."
- **VR's paradox**: Breaks the screen tradition by requiring the viewer to physically move — but simultaneously imprisons the body more completely than ever (Sutherland's "Sword of Damocles," head-mounted displays tethered by cables).
- **Alternative tradition**: Frescoes, mosaics, panoramas, human-scale sculptures — representations at the same scale as the viewer's world, encouraging movement rather than immobility. VR continues this simulation tradition but breaks continuity between physical and virtual space.

### Military origins
- The computer screen was born from military surveillance, not public entertainment. Radar → SAGE (MIT Lincoln Lab) → Sutherland's Sketchpad → interactive computer graphics → VR.
- The light pen (1949) is the precursor of the mouse.
