# Introduction to Korean Edition of The Language of New Media

## Summary

A short introduction for the Korean translation of *The Language of New Media* that reframes the book's approach through the metaphor of **remix**. Manovich identifies three types of remix operating in contemporary culture: postmodern remix (remixing cultural content), globalization remix (remixing national traditions), and new media remix (remixing culture and computers). He argues that "information aesthetics" will follow a different logic from industrial modernism.

## Key Arguments

- **Korea as contradiction**: Korea is a quintessential "new media civilization" yet remains "traditional" and "hierarchical" — values that appear to contradict open source and hypertext ideologies. Such tensions are exactly what the book examines.
- **Hybrid languages**: The "languages" of new media are always hybrids incorporating memories and techniques of established cultural forms (cinema, theatre, books) together with techniques from digital networked computers.
- **Industrial modernism took 100 years**: From the engine (early 1800s) to the cultural "superstructure" of geometric forms, bold typography, and abstract compositions (1920s). We may be in a similarly early stage for information aesthetics.
- **Against tabula rasa**: Industrial modernism wanted to erase the old. Information aesthetics works differently — it creatively combines old and new, as exemplified by Barcelona's hybrid energy.
- **Three remixes**: (1) Postmodern — remixing cultural contents within a given medium; (2) Globalization — remixing national traditions and "global international" style; (3) New media — remixing culture and computers, "the remix between the interfaces of various cultural forms and the new software techniques."
