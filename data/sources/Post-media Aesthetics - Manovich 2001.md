# Post-media Aesthetics

## Summary

A programmatic essay arguing that the concept of "medium" has become meaningless and proposing a replacement framework: **post-media aesthetics** based on concepts from software culture. Manovich sketches a new critical vocabulary organized around data structures, algorithms, software, interfaces, and **information behavior** — and argues that software itself should become a primary object of cultural analysis.

## Key Arguments

- **Medium in crisis**: Three developments destroyed the traditional typology of mediums: (1) proliferation of new art forms in the 1960s (installation, performance, conceptual art); (2) addition of mass media technologies (photography, film, video) that confused art/mass culture distinctions; (3) digital revolution that erased material differences between media.
- **Digital attack**: Digital representation + common editing tools (copy, paste, morph, filter, composite) erased the difference between photography and painting, film and animation. The Web dissolved mass/limited distribution distinctions.
- **Post-media aesthetics program**: (1) Categories should describe how objects organize data and structure user experience; (2) categories should not be tied to any storage/communication medium; (3) adopt computer/network metaphors (interface, bandwidth, stream, compress); (4) replace "medium" with "software" — ask what information operations a medium allows.
- **Information behavior**: A new concept describing particular ways of accessing and processing information — from organizing computer files to reading literature to navigating a city. "The history of art is also the history of new information interfaces developed by artists, and the new information behaviors developed by users."
- **Software as critical object**: Post-author → text → reader trajectory should add a fourth component: **software** used by author and reader. Software shapes what a text is and what can be done with it.
- **The office and the club**: The same machine (digital computer) enables both cognitive work and affective bodily experience. Data moves both brains and bodies.
