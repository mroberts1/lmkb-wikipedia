# Cinema by Numbers

## Summary

An essay on the convergence of cinema and computer, using Konrad Zuse's punched film tape as an origin story for digital cinema. Manovich analyzes new media's material logic (automation, variability) and argues that despite this radical new logic, commercial cinema's visual language remains unchanged. He celebrates Vuk Cosic's ASCII films as artworks that "perform" the new status of media as digital data.

## Key Arguments

- **Zuse's film as origin**: Zuse used discarded 35mm movie film for his computer's punched tape — binary code superimposed over cinematic images. "In a technological remake of the Oedipal complex, a son murders his father."
- **Convergence**: Media and computer merge — all existing media become numerical data. The computer becomes a media processor, returning to its origins as Jacquard's loom.
- **Automation and variability**: New media's key properties — automation removes human intentionality; variability means media exists in potentially infinite versions. This corresponds to post-industrial "production on demand."
- **Why no new forms?**: Despite radical new material logic, commercial cinema uses computers to preserve old cinematic language (hiding digital manipulation). Perhaps "net.capitalism is still capitalism" and the information age hasn't truly arrived yet.
- **Cosic's ASCII films**: Where Zuse erases iconic code in favor of binary and Lucas hides binary code under iconic images, Cosic reveals both — "the code and the image coexist." His work "performs" the new status of media as data.
- **Media translation as culture**: Since the 1960s, media translation (film→video→digital→format→format) has been at the core of culture. Warhol and Lichtenstein made this the basis of their art.
