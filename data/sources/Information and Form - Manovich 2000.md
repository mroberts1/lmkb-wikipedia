# Information and Form

## Summary

A short essay-review of the Electrolobby at Ars Electronica 2000, framed around the fundamental tension between **form** and **information** in the shift from modernism to what Manovich calls "informationalism." Modernism sought good form (Malevich, Mondrian, Arp); information society produces dynamic, immaterial networks that resist solidifying into objects.

## Key Arguments

- **Form vs. information**: "What search for good form was for modernism, information networking is for our own society." Modernism produced solid objects; information culture produces dynamic processes that never thicken into fixed forms.
- **Translating information into form**: The central challenge — how to represent, document, and support social networking as cultural practice in its own right. Museums and the art market are designed for material objects, not immaterial networks.
- **Electrolobby as experiment**: TNC network's Electrolobby attempted to translate net culture's logic into spatial-temporal form — ambient, peak-free, parallel, small-increment activity rather than linear succession and big events.
- **Net culture's logic**: Things happen in parallel and in small increments; booths are often empty because participants are elsewhere; checking email becomes the primary activity. Perhaps this *is* the right form for information culture.
- **inFORMation**: The word itself implies a hidden form-making impulse — information processes leave material residues (websites, manifestos, email postings) that can be forced into material traces.
