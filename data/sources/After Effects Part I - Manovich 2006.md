# After Effects, or Velvet Revolution. Part I

## Summary

The first part of a two-part series analyzing the "Velvet Revolution" in moving image culture (1993–1998) — the gradual, almost invisible transformation during which previously separate media (cinematography, graphics, photography, animation, 3D animation, typography) started combining in numerous ways. Manovich centers After Effects software as both symbol and material foundation of this revolution, analyzing compositing, layers/transparency, and the shift from time-based to composition-based moving images.

## Key Arguments

- **Velvet Revolution**: By analogy with Czechoslovakia's peaceful 1989 liberation, the transformation of moving image aesthetics was gradual and non-violent — yet fundamental. "The 'pure' moving image media became an exception and hybrid media became the norm."
- **Huyssen's prediction realized**: In 1986, Huyssen imagined "computerized memory banks" enabling postmodern pastiche — but the technology didn't exist until the mid-1990s when After Effects and similar tools became affordable.
- **Media remixability**: "What gets remixed is not only content of different media but their fundamental techniques, working methods, and assumptions." United in software, all media form a new metamedium.
- **Layers and transparency revolution**: Before digital compositing, combining multiple media within the same frame was extremely difficult (optical printing, video keying). After Effects allowed hundreds of layers, each with independent transparency — analogous to multitrack audio recording.
- **From time-based to composition-based**: After Effects reconceptualized moving images. Film/video editing treated a frame as a "black box." After Effects treats each visual element as an independent object in a 2D composition. "A film is re-conceptualized as a graphic design that can change over time."
- **Interface remix**: After Effects' own interface remixes working methods from cell animation (transparent layers), stop-motion animation (frame-by-frame manipulation of independent elements), and graphic design (real-time visual feedback).
