# Database as a Symbolic Form

## Summary

One of Manovich's most famous and influential essays, later a chapter in *The Language of New Media*. Following Panofsky's analysis of linear perspective as the "symbolic form" of the modern age, Manovich argues that the **database** is the new symbolic form of the computer age — a new way to structure experience of ourselves and the world. The essay maps the opposition between database and narrative onto the semiological categories of paradigm and syntagm, arguing that new media reverses their traditional relationship.

## Key Arguments

- **Database as dominant cultural form**: After the novel and cinema privileged narrative, the computer age introduces database. Most new media objects are collections of items with equal significance, not stories with development.
- **Data structures and algorithms**: Computer ontology reduces the world to two complementary halves — data structures and algorithms. CD-ROMs and websites manifest data structures; computer games manifest algorithms.
- **Database vs. narrative**: "Database and narrative are natural enemies." Database represents the world as an unordered list; narrative creates cause-and-effect trajectories. In new media, database is the "unmarked term" — all new media objects are databases underneath.
- **Paradigm/syntagm reversal**: In traditional media, syntagm (the actual narrative) is explicit while paradigm (the database of choices) is implicit. New media reverses this: the database (paradigm) is given material existence while narrative (syntagm) is dematerialized.
- **Interface to a database**: Creating a work in new media = constructing an interface to a database. The same database can support multiple interfaces and trajectories.
- **Database cinema**: Greenaway wraps minimal narrative around databases using number systems. Vertov's *Man with a Movie Camera* is the supreme example — traversing a database in a particular order to construct an argument, merging database and narrative into a new form.
