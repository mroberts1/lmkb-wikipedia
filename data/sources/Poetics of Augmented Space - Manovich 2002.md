# The Poetics of Augmented Space

## Summary

A major essay (updated 2005) proposing **augmented space** as the key concept for understanding 21st-century spatial culture. Manovich argues that after the 1990s' fascination with virtual space, attention has shifted to physical space overlaid with dynamic information — via surveillance, cellspace/mobile media, and electronic displays. He reframes augmentation as an aesthetic and cultural problem with deep historical roots, not merely a technological one.

## Key Arguments

- **From virtual to augmented**: The 1990s were about virtual space (VR, cyberspace, dot-coms). The 2000s are about physical space filled with electronic information. The icon shifts from a VR user in virtual space to a person checking email on a cell phone at the airport.
- **Augmented space defined**: Physical space overlaid with dynamically changing, often multimedia, often localized information. Three key technologies: video surveillance (extracting data from space), cellspace/mobile media (delivering data to space), electronic displays (presenting data in space).
- **Augmentation ↔ monitoring**: The delivery of information to users and the extraction of information about users are in symbiotic relationship. "Augmented space is also monitored space."
- **From Panopticon to information theory**: Foucault's Panopticon (geometry of the visible, binary visible/invisible) no longer describes spatial logic. Instead, Shannon's information theory (signal + noise, continuous values) captures the reality of augmented space — signals always accompanied by noise, occupying positions on a continuum.
- **Augmentation as cultural practice**: Not new — Cardiff's audio walks, Libeskind's Jewish Museum Berlin, and the history of art from Tatlin's reliefs through minimalism to installation art all address the problem of overlaying data onto physical space.
- **White cube trajectory**: 20th-century art moves from 2D surface → 3D space (Tatlin → Lissitzky → minimalism → installation). Augmented space is the next step: placing a user inside a space filled with dynamic, interactive, contextual data.
