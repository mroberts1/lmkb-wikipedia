# Information as an Aesthetic Event

## Summary

An essay analyzing the shift in user interface design from the "invisible interface" paradigm to treating interaction as an explicitly aesthetic **event** and **experience**. Manovich traces this through OS 9 → OS X (Aqua), LG Chocolate phone, and iPhone, connecting it to broader trends of aestheticization of information tools, experience economy, and supermodernist design.

## Key Arguments

- **Aestheticization of information tools**: Beginning ~1996-98 (Wallpaper magazine, Colette store, iMac, Guggenheim Bilbao), information tools shifted from cold/business associations to friendly, playful, fashionable, emotionally satisfying. "Form follows function" replaced by "form follows emotion."
- **Interaction as event**: Designers no longer try to hide interfaces. "The interaction is treated as an event — as opposed to 'non-event,' as in the previous 'invisible interface' paradigm." Using devices is now conceived as carefully orchestrated experience.
- **OS 9 vs. OS X**: OS 9's strict geometry, grays, and business-like icons spoke of modernist office culture. OS X Aqua's colorful 3D icons, shadows, transparency, animations, and playful Dock effects redefined computer use as explicitly aesthetic experience.
- **Three stages of interfaces**: Command-line (delivers goods/functionality) → classical GUI (adds service) → post-OS X sensual interfaces (delivers experiences). Correlates to three stages of consumer economy: goods → services → experiences (Pine & Gilmore).
- **LG Chocolate as Gesamtkunstwerk**: When switched on, the phone unfolds a multimedia drama — invisible buttons appear glowing red, screen plays animation, phone vibrates at the exact moment the logo appears. Engages sight, sound, touch, and evokes taste through name/color.
- **Supermodernism and disappearance**: Apple's post-iMac designs (Titanium PowerBook, iPod, Mac Mini) adopted a minimal aesthetics of disappearance — technology wanting to fade into the background. This parallels Hans Ibelings's "supermodernism" in architecture (Jean Nouvel, Herzog & De Meuron). Both create unique sensorial experiences while minimizing visual presence.
