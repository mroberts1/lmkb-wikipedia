# The Engineering of Vision and the Aesthetics of Computer Art

## Summary

Manovich argues that the rise of computer graphics, human-computer interfaces, and computer vision must be understood as part of the shift to **post-industrial society**, where vision becomes the primary instrument of labor. He traces a lineage from the 1920s avant-garde's desire to "engineer vision" through WWII radar operations to contemporary VR, proposing that the computer artist should be understood in relation to the computer graphics industry rather than in isolation.

## Key Arguments

- **Avant-garde as proto-engineering of vision**: Constructivist design minimized visual waste (block letters, no decoration); Vertov's montage "forced the eye" to see efficiently; Eisenstein tried to measure the impact of art in quantifiable units ("attractions").
- **Post-industrial shift**: The radar operator of WWII is the prototypical figure of the new perceptual labor — vision replaces manual work as the primary mode of production. This leads to massive investment in vision research.
- **Three developments from radar**:
  1. Human engineering / human factors — designing displays and controls around the limits of human perception
  2. Interactive computer graphics — developed to process radar information more efficiently (SAGE, Lincoln Lab)
  3. Computer vision — automating sight entirely, removing the human from the loop
- **Proposed aesthetics for computer art**:
  - If art is anti-productive, the computer artist should design "bad interfaces" — inefficient, confusing, chaotic
  - Alternatively, redefine "computer art" as "art for computers" — art optimized for machine perception. A computer critic might prefer images that compress well, championing Malevich as the artist of compression aesthetics.
