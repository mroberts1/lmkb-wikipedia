# Macro-media and Micro-media

## Summary

A short, prescient essay identifying two divergent trajectories for digital media beyond the "broadband" frontier of television-quality video. **Macro-media** envisions ultra-high-resolution wall-filling displays with multi-frame narratives and mixed media. **Micro-media** identifies a persistent counter-trajectory of minimalist, low-resolution media that keeps migrating to new platforms (cell phones, PDAs) as older platforms gain resolution.

## Key Arguments

- **Web is not television**: While industry obsesses over replicating "VHS quality" video on the Web, what makes the Web genuinely new is its non-television properties — unlimited streams, search, hyperlinks, multiple simultaneous playback, automatic indexing.
- **Macro-media**: When bandwidth and resolution cease to be constraints, extra screen space can be filled with multiple video frames (anticipating Figgis's *Timecode*) or surrounding media (as Bloomberg TV already does). "The future films and soap operas may look more like Bloomberg TV and less like *Gone with the Wind*."
- **Micro-media**: A persistent phenomenon where low-resolution media keeps reappearing on new platforms. Pre-rendered 3D animation limitations of the 1980s returned as VRML bandwidth limitations in the 1990s. Rich HTML was stripped back down to WAP for phones. Cell phone screens have roughly the resolution of 1970s-80s game consoles.
- **Body as limit**: The evolution toward richer media is limited by human body size — hand dimensions and eye/ear resolution. Until direct neural interfaces arrive, micro-media is permanent.
- **Migration pattern**: Micro-media doesn't disappear — it migrates. "Having been wiped out by technological evolution on one platform, ultra-low-resolution computer games return at a later time, on another platform."
