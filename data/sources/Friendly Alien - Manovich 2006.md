# Friendly Alien: Object and Interface

## Summary

A short essay on the relationship between physical objects and digital interfaces, using artist Miltos Manetas's paintings and visits to the Colette store and Eindhoven design school as springboards. Manovich argues that the interface is a "friendly alien" — integrated into daily life but still fundamentally discontinuous with the material objects that house it.

## Key Arguments

- **Manetas's trajectory**: 1990s paintings showed technology as alien and immersive (cables multiplying, users "sucked in"). 2000s paintings show technology as playful, integrated, and fashion-like — "a pet which surprises us."
- **Technology as design object**: At Colette, cell phones and PDAs occupied the center display, acquiring the same status as perfume and photography books. "They were no longer 'technology.' Instead, they became simply 'objects.'"
- **The discontinuity problem**: Techno-objects contain interfaces — screens, buttons — and behind the screen lives a whole separate world. The physical boundary of the screen creates "visual and psychological feeling of discontinuity." PSP fashion cases illustrate this — their design logic has nothing to do with the screen interface world.
- **Form and interface must accommodate each other**: As ubiquitous computing spreads, "the forms of all these objects that previously lived 'outside of information' now have to address the likely presence of interfaces somewhere on them."
- **Smart objects as solution**: Eindhoven student projects (electronic canopy, video mirror, interactive light column) show surfaces becoming both output and input media, "bringing together the physical and the screen-like in surprising ways."
