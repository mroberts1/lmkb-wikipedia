# The Paradoxes of Digital Photography

## Summary

Manovich argues against the common claim that digital imaging represents a radical rupture with photography. Instead, the logic of digital photography is **paradoxical**: it simultaneously tears apart and reinforces the codes of photographic culture. He systematically dismantles William Mitchell's (*The Reconfigured Eye*) claims about fundamental physical differences between analog and digital images, then reframes 3D computer graphics as "socialist realism."

## Key Arguments

### Against Mitchell's three distinctions:
1. **Copy degradation**: In theory, digital copies are perfect. In practice, lossy compression (JPEG) means digital images *lose more information* between copies than traditional photographs. Lossy compression is becoming the foundation of digital visual culture, not an aberration.
2. **Finite vs. infinite information**: True, digital images have fixed pixel counts. But modern scanners (1200-2400 DPI) can capture far more detail than anyone would ever need, nullifying the practical distinction.
3. **Inherent mutability**: Mitchell identifies "straight" photography as the norm that digital disrupts. But straight photography was never the norm — Soviet retouching, advertising design, and photomontage traditions always coexisted. "Normal photography never existed."

### Socialist Realism of *Jurassic Park*:
- What CG achieves is not realism but **photorealism** — simulating the look of a camera image, not reality itself.
- Synthetic images are actually **too real** — hyperreal, free of depth-of-field artifacts, grain, and noise. They represent the vision of a cyborg, not a human.
- To integrate CG with film footage, the perfection must be *degraded* (noise added, edges softened, resolution reduced).
- This parallels **Socialist Realism**: projecting a perfect future onto familiar present reality, blending the two without ever depicting the future directly.
