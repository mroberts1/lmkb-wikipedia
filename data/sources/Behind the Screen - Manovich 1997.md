# Behind the Screen / Russian New Media

## Summary

Manovich surveys Russian/post-communist approaches to new media art, arguing they offer valuable alternatives to Western default thematics. He identifies two main strategies: Moscow conceptualism's ironic suspicion of technology (Shulgin, Prigov), and a rethinking of "screen culture" as a historical tradition connecting old and new media (Lialina, Tobreluts).

## Key Arguments

- **Post-communist suspicion**: Technology always breaks down; signal always contains noise; interactivity is manipulation. These insights, forged under totalitarianism, align naturally with information theory (Shannon).
- **Prigov's machine translation**: Using business software to translate Pushkin Russian→Finnish→English, declaring the errors a new work of art. Noise as creative signal.
- **Shulgin's meta-art**: WWWArt Medal awards "art feeling" to non-art web pages. The Web blurs art/non-art more than any previous medium.
- **Screen culture**: Russian thinkers expand the screen beyond display surface to bridge between physical/imaginary, link between subject and audiovisual stream, window onto alternative reality. This connects old and new media.
- **Lialina's "My Boyfriend Came Back from War"**: Uses browser frames-within-frames to bridge parallel montage and interactive hypertext.
- **Tobreluts' compositing**: Seamlessly composites radically different realities within perspective-matched interior spaces — between MTV juxtaposition and Hollywood seamless illusion.
- **Russian painting tradition**: Unlike the West (painting as material object), Russian artists maintain the painting as window onto parallel reality (Bulatov, Kabakov).
