# From the Externalization of the Psyche to the Implantation of Technology

## Summary

Manovich traces a trajectory from the **externalization of the mind** — the recurrent modern claim that new visual technologies objectify mental processes — to the emerging possibility of **implanting technology back inside the body**. He argues that externalization served the needs of mass society for standardization: making private mental states public and regulable.

## Key Arguments

- **Galton's composites** (1877): Composite photographs were claimed to materialize Platonic abstractions — giving "abstract ideas" concrete, touchable, reproducible form.
- **Münsterberg on cinema** (1916): Film techniques map one-to-one onto mental functions — close-up = attention, cut-back = memory. "The photoplay obeys the laws of the mind rather than those of the outer world."
- **Eisenstein's *Capital* project**: Montage can provoke and direct dialectical thinking itself. Not merely representing ideas but forcing the viewer through the *process* of Marxist reasoning. Film as mass pedagogy for illiterate populations.
- **The isomorphism assumption**: Throughout modernity, new visual technologies prompt claims that external images are equivalent to internal mental representations. This assumption is "highly problematic" (the mind doesn't contain pictures) yet irresistible.
- **Technologies model the mind**: The clock (17th c.), the motor (19th c.), the computer (20th c.) — each era's dominant technology provides the metaphor for mental processes. Cognitive psychology's models of mental imagery suspiciously resemble computer imaging systems.
- **Freud and Galton**: Freud explicitly compared dream condensation to Galton's composite photography technique.
- **From outside to inside**: The trajectory reverses — from projecting the mind outward (cinema, VR) to implanting technology inward (brain-computer interfaces, neural implants, the neuro-cyborg).
