# Introduction to Info-Aesthetics

## Summary

The draft introduction to Manovich's planned book *Info-Aesthetics*, which investigates the cultural forms emerging from information society. He asks: if information processing is the defining characteristic of our world, what is its effect on the forms we design? The essay covers information visualization, the convergence of form and interface in ubiquitous computing, and argues that the exponential growth of available media is one of the main "pressure points" on contemporary culture.

## Key Arguments

- **Information processing as everyday reality**: Regardless of profession, white-collar workers are all "processing information." This extends beyond work into leisure — search engines, email, digital photos, GPS tracks. "Information society is where most citizens of the developed and developing world live today."
- **Two lines of influence**: (1) The centrality of dealing with information may affect aesthetic preferences across all cultural fields; (2) Most forms are designed on computers, which shapes what designers produce. "Information processing acts both as a force outside a form and as the very method through which forms are being designed."
- **Form meets interface**: As computation becomes incorporated into our lived environment (ubiquitous computing), interfaces leave stand-alone devices and appear on all kinds of surfaces and objects. "Form follows interface" is not quite right — rather, the two must accommodate each other.
- **Smart objects**: Eindhoven Technical University student projects — electronic canopy, video mirror, light column — show different ways objects, interfaces, and displays can be integrated, bringing together "the physical and the screen-like."
- **Screen forms**: Beyond material forms, there are "screen forms" — ways to structure data for human users (cinematic narrative, interactive visualization, search engines, UI).
- **Scale as new critical vocabulary**: Beyond qualitative categories (authorship, media type, ideology), we need to consider something purely quantitative: "the dramatic increase in the scale of media available." Standard interface to culture is now a search engine. We deal not with "old media" or "new media" but "more media."
