# Data Visualization as New Abstraction and Anti-Sublime

## Summary

An essay analyzing data visualization and mapping as a new cultural form, arguing that it constitutes both a "new abstraction" and an "anti-sublime." Manovich examines visualization art projects (John Simon, Lisa Jevbratt, RSG's Carnivore) and proposes that data visualization reverses the Romantic sublime by mapping phenomena beyond human perception into manageable visual forms.

## Key Arguments

- **Visualization vs. mapping**: Visualization transforms non-visual quantified data into visual representation; mapping is the broader operation of converting one data set into another. Visualization is a subset of mapping.
- **Politics of mapping**: Who decides what dimensions to select, what mapping to use, what interface to provide — these questions are as important as traditional questions about the politics of media representation.
- **Data modernism**: Just as early 20th-century modernists mapped visual chaos of the metropolis into simple geometric images, data visualization artists transform informational chaos into orderly forms. Data visualization is "a new abstraction."
- **Anti-sublime**: Romantic art was concerned with the sublime — the unrepresentable. Data visualization is the exact opposite: it maps phenomena beyond human senses into representations at human scale. "The macro and the micro, the infinite and the endless are mapped into manageable visual objects."
- **Problem of motivation**: The fundamental weakness of mapping projects is arbitrary choice — since computers allow mapping anything into anything else, why this particular mapping? Libeskind's Jewish Museum illustrates this tension between rational data collection and arbitrary projection.
- **Beyond anti-sublime**: The real challenge for data art is not mapping abstract data into something beautiful (economists and scientists already do this) but representing the subjective experience of living in a data society — "being immersed in data."
