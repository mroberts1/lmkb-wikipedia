# The Practice of Everyday (Media) Life

## Summary

The final essay in the collection, analyzing the explosion of user-generated content and social media through Michel de Certeau's framework of strategies vs. tactics. Manovich argues that Web 2.0 has reversed the relationship: corporate strategies now mimic people's tactics (customization, remix, bricolage), while people's tactical creativity has become visible, permanent, and mappable. He also introduces concepts of "media conversations" and asks whether professional art can survive extreme democratization.

## Key Arguments

- **From mass consumption to mass production**: The shift from most people consuming professional content to many producing their own. But only 0.5-1.5% of users on major sites actually contribute content. The "long tail" means every item finds some audience.
- **Tactics became strategies**: De Certeau (1980) distinguished institutional strategies from individual tactics (bricolage, customization, remix). Today, companies design products explicitly for customization (GUI, iGoogle, Facebook apps, Nike custom shoes, Scion cars). "The logic of tactics has now become the logic of strategies."
- **Strategies became tactics**: Social media companies' strategies are flexible, constantly changing, designed for "hackability and remixability" — qualities de Certeau attributed to tactics. Meanwhile, user-produced content (AMVs, blogs) becomes permanent, mappable, and viewable — losing the transience de Certeau attributed to tactics.
- **Critical questions about UGC**: Is mass production of cultural objects by users progressive? Or does it constitute a further stage of "culture industry" — with 21st-century prosumers "passionately imitating" commercial media rather than just consuming it?
- **Media conversations**: New communication forms where content, opinion, and conversation can't be separated. Conversations *through* media (video responses on YouTube) and conversations *around* media (comments on Flickr photos). Content becomes tokens for initiating/maintaining conversation.
- **Art after Web 2.0**: The real challenge to professional art is not amateur content quality but "the dynamics of Web 2.0 culture — its constant innovation, its energy, and its unpredictability."
