# Assembling Reality: Myths of Computer Graphics

## Summary

Manovich tests three influential theories of cinematic realism — Bazin's idealist teleology, Comolli's Marxist structuralism, and Bordwell/Staiger's industrial model — against the history of 3D computer graphics. He finds each partially applicable but ultimately insufficient, arguing that CG realism is fundamentally different from photographic realism: it is **partial, uneven, and driven by specific sponsors** (the Pentagon and Hollywood).

## Key Arguments

- **Bazin applied to CG**: The history of computer graphics *does* look like Bazin's evolutionary narrative — from wireframes to textures to interactive VR, asymptotically approaching "total realism." But this is misleading.
- **Comolli's substitution model**: Early CG compensated for schematic rendering with exaggerated depth cues (grids, rapid in-depth movement). As rendering improved, these depth codes disappeared and lateral movement in shallow space took over — confirming Comolli's thesis that realistic codes substitute for one another rather than accumulating.
- **Bordwell/Staiger's industrial model**: The competitive pressure of software innovation drives the substitution of codes. Companies must showcase the latest algorithms; old effects become commodity and lose distinction value.
- **Uneven realism**: CG can simulate plastic and metal but not cloth or leather; a human face but not hair. This unevenness reflects which problems the Pentagon (landscapes for flight simulators) and Hollywood (synthetic actors, special effects) funded.
- **Icons of mimesis**: Researchers privilege subjects that culturally connote mastery of representation — grapes (Zeuxis), moving nature (smoke, fire, waves) — to overcompensate for the inability to simulate arbitrary scenes.
