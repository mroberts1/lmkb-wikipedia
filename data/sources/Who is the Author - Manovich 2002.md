# Who is the Author?

## Summary

A systematic taxonomy of new authorship models enabled by new media: collaboration, interactivity-as-miscommunication, authorship-as-selection, company-user collaboration, author-software collaboration, remixing, sampling, open source, and brand-as-author. Manovich argues that remix culture calls for a new critical vocabulary that can work across media.

## Key Arguments

- **Nine models of authorship**: (1) Network collaboration; (2) Interactivity as miscommunication between author and user; (3) Authorship as selection from menus; (4) Company-user collaboration (Doom modding, Sims); (5) Author-software collaboration (AI/AL); (6) Remixing (from Moulton's chaste disco remixes to aggressive sampling); (7) Sampling as industrialized montage/collage; (8) Open Source (license and kernel concepts applicable to culture); (9) Brand as author (corporate vs. individual brands).
- **Remixing vs. appropriation**: "Remixing" is better than "appropriation" because it implies systematic reworking, not just transfer of a sign from one sphere to another. While remixing is officially accepted in music, in other media it's considered copyright violation.
- **Open source concepts for culture**: The ideas of **license** (specifying what modifications are allowed) and **kernel** (the core that shouldn't be fundamentally changed) can be directly applied to cultural authorship. OPUS project by Raqs Media Collective implements this as "rescension."
- **Need for cross-media vocabulary**: Electronic music theory (mixing, sampling, synthesis), literary theory (intertext, paratext, hyperlinking), and visual culture (montage, collage, appropriation) each have useful terms that should be correlated into a unified critical vocabulary.
