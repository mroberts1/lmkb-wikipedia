# Social Data Browsing

## Summary

A short essay on Golan Levin's *The Dumpster* (2006, commissioned by Tate Modern) as exemplifying a new genre Manovich calls the "social data browser" — a representation that links individual experience and larger social groupings without subsuming one in the other. He frames this through the historical paradox of 19th-century art (Flaubert, Tolstoy) and sociology (Comte, Durkheim) developing simultaneously but in opposite directions.

## Key Arguments

- **Art's limitation**: Representational art has depicted individuals rather than groups. Even socialist realism shows individual human beings. When artists try to represent large groups, the result is a crowd where individual differences are lost. This may be an essential characteristic of images — their inability to represent abstract concepts and logical relationships.
- **Computer techniques as solution**: Computers allow switching between multiple views, traversing data at different scales, and mining large amounts of data in real time. This enables representations that link individual and social without subsuming one in the other.
- **The Dumpster as social data browser**: 20,000 blog postings about teenage breakups, subjected to algorithmic analysis to derive metadata (reasons, emotions, demographics). Navigation is simultaneously horizontal (jumping between cases), vertical (individual to pattern), and diagonal.
- **Tolstoy meets Durkheim**: The subjective experience and the social facts are brought together via information architecture. "The seemingly incompatible points of view of Tolstoy and Durkheim are brought together."
- **Group portrait for the data mining age**: The group was created by searching through digital traces people leave online. Ordering is the result of mathematical analysis. "We are invited to mine the data."
