# The Labor of Perception

## Summary

Manovich argues that the shift from industrial to post-industrial society entailed a parallel shift from disciplines of bodily efficiency (Taylorism, the "human motor") to disciplines of mental efficiency (cognitive science, human factors engineering). The **radar operator** of WWII is the pivotal figure who inaugurated this transformation, standing at the origin of cognitive science, human-computer interaction, and the broader regime of perceptual labor.

## Key Arguments

- **Benjamin updated**: Benjamin saw the factory conveyor belt and cinema as sharing the same rhythm of perceptual shocks. The post-industrial equivalents are the computer game and the military simulator — and they now literally share the same technology (SIMNET → Battletech Center; LucasArts + Hughes Aircraft).
- **From human motor to human information processor**: Rabinbach's *The Human Motor* showed how thermodynamics made the worker's body legible as an energy system. The equivalent for post-industrial society is information theory making the mind legible as a data-processing system.
- **Taylorism vs. European science of work**: Taylor aimed for *maximum* productivity regardless of worker exhaustion; Europeans aimed for *optimum* productivity. Both were concerned with the body. Cognitive science inherits the same logic but applies it to the mind.
- **Herbert Simon** bridged Taylor's management science and cognitive science. **Jerome Bruner** explicitly linked the "cognitive revolution" to the demands of the "post-industrial revolution."
- **Applied experimental psychology**: Born from WWII, when radar operators, anti-aircraft gunners, and pilots found themselves in the exact same situations as subjects in 19th-century psychophysical experiments (detection, identification, recognition, reaction time).
- **From radar to VR**: The radar screen was the first modern visual human-machine interface; VR gear is the latest. Both are products of "human engineering" — designing displays around the capacities and limits of human perception.
