# On Totalitarian Interactivity

## Summary

A provocative short essay arguing that interactive media is a form of manipulation and mental control, not liberation. Writing as a "post-communist subject," Manovich contrasts Western enthusiasm for interactivity as democracy with the Eastern European view of it as totalitarian control. He proposes that interactive media externalizes mental processes (association, memory, problem-solving) and asks users to mistake someone else's mental structure for their own.

## Key Arguments

- **Post-communist critique**: For the West, interactivity = democracy. For post-communist subjects (Shulgin, Groys, Manovich), interactivity = manipulation — interactive installations resemble CIA/KGB psychological laboratories.
- **Literal vs. psychological interaction**: All art was already "interactive" — requiring the viewer to fill in missing information, form hypotheses, make associations. Computer interactivity reduces this rich psychological process to physical button-pressing.
- **Externalization of mental life**: Interactive media externalizes and objectifies association, memory, and problem-solving. Hyperlinks objectify human thinking (connecting ideas/images/memories). Users are asked to follow pre-programmed associations instead of their own.
- **New interpellation**: "We are asked to mistake the structure of somebody else's mind for our own." Industrial technologies (cinema, fashion) asked identification with someone's *body*. Interactive media asks identification with someone's *mental structure*.
