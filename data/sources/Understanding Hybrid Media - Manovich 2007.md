# Understanding Hybrid Media

## Summary

A polished synthesis of ideas from the After Effects essays, presenting Manovich's theory of **deep remixability** and **media hybridity** through analysis of works by Jeremy Blake, Takeshi Murata, and Ann Lislegaard. He argues that software-based production created a metamedium where previously separate media techniques interact to create genuinely new species, and that the resulting "aesthetics of continuity" — where all constants become variables — defines contemporary visual culture.

## Key Arguments

- **The invisible revolution**: In the late 1990s, previously separate media started combining. By ~1998, hybrid media became the norm. This revolution received no critical attention because no new media per se were created — "the visual language of these images was now very different."
- **Deep remixability defined**: "What gets remixed today is not only content from different media but also their fundamental techniques, working methods, and ways of representation and expression." Distinguished from simple content remix (anime music videos, music remixes).
- **From media to algorithms**: Computerization virtualized media techniques — "extracting them from their particular physical medium of origin and turning them into algorithms." Techniques become "supercharged": their range extended, controls made explicit, quantifiable, and programmable.
- **The variable form**: Software represents all phenomena as sets of variables that can change continuously. Animation software interfaces literally invite designers to animate every dimension. The result: "constant change on many visual dimensions" as the norm, not the exception.
- **Continuity turn**: Both temporal visual form (motion graphics) and spatial form (architecture) started exploring continuous change and transformation. Previously marginal aesthetics (Kiesler's Continuous House, Fischinger's Motion Painting) became the norm.
- **Blake, Murata, Lislegaard**: Three different articulations of hybrid aesthetics — Blake's continuous layering/fading, Murata's permanent metamorphosis between live and abstract, Lislegaard's variable architectural spaces. "Different media become compatible but can preserve their distinct identities."
- **Architecture parallel**: When architects adopted animation software (Alias/Maya) in the 1990s, spline-based modeling pushed thinking toward continuous curved forms. Yokohama Port Terminal as paradigmatic example.
