# To Lie and To Act: Cinema and Telecommunication

## Summary

Manovich proposes two fundamental axes structuring the history of visual representations: **deception** (creating fake realities) and **action** (enabling manipulation of reality through representations). He traces the evolution of deception technologies from Potemkin's Villages through cinema, video keying, and digital compositing, then examines telepresence as a technology of action.

## Key Arguments

### To Lie (Technologies of Deception)
- **Potemkin's Villages** → Cinema → Video → Digital: each generation expands the possibilities for creating fake realities.
- **Cinema**: Overcame the materiality of the viewer's body by substituting recorded images for unmediated sight. Editing creates coherent spaces from disconnected fragments. Two types of montage: montage within a shot (rear projection) and temporal montage (cutting between shots).
- **Video keying**: The shift from temporal montage to routine montage within a shot. Weather maps, news broadcasts — electronic keying openly presents clashing spaces rather than hiding the seam.
- **Digital compositing**: Hundreds of layers (film, CG, matte paintings, archival footage) combined seamlessly. The paradigm shifts from creating convincing individual elements to **blending them at the edges** — "the borders where different realities come together is the new arena."
- Rybczynski's *Steps* (1987): Keying tourists into Eisenstein's *Potemkin* — a brilliant meta-commentary connecting analog, electronic, and digital fake-reality technologies.

### To Act (Technologies of Action)
- **Telepresence** is more radical than VR: VR gives control over virtual worlds (data in memory); telepresence gives control over *physical reality* at a distance.
- **Image-instruments** (Latour): Perspective was never just representation — it enabled manipulation of reality through representations. Maps, x-rays, architectural drawings are instruments for action.
- **Telecommunication** as the key enabler: Electronic transmission makes instantaneous both the creation of representations and the manipulation of objects through those representations. "Real-time remote control."
- Eco's definition updated: "A sign is something which can be used to tell a lie" → "A sign is something which can be used to teleact."
