# Cinema and Digital Media

## Summary

A compact essay arguing that cinema and the digital computer are not separate histories but deeply intertwined — cinema literally gave birth to the computer, prepared audiences for digital media concepts, and established the representational conventions that digital simulation now mimics.

## Key Arguments

### Cinema gives birth to a computer
- The Jacquard loom (c. 1800) — a programmable machine that synthesized *images* (woven patterns) before Babbage adapted the concept for numerical computation. Ada Augusta: "The Analytical Engine weaves algebraical patterns just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves."
- Konrad Zuse built his early computer using discarded 35mm movie film as punched tape — "the iconic code of cinema is discarded in favor of the more efficient binary one."
- Digital media = the convergence of two parallel histories: the computer (numerical data processing) and modern media (storage of images, sounds, text in material forms).

### Cinema prepares digital media
- **Sampling**: Cinema samples time 24 times per second — the crucial conceptual break from continuous to discrete representation. Digitization merely quantifies what cinema already made discrete.
- **Random access**: Pre-cinema devices (Zoetrope, Edison's cylindrical apparatus with 42,000 tiny images) mapped time onto 2D space, preparing the concept of non-linear access.

### Simulation
- What digital simulation achieves is not realism but **photorealism** — simulating the camera's image of reality, not reality itself.
- "Conceptually, digitally simulated worlds already appeared with the first films of the Lumières and Méliès in the 1890s. It is they who invented digital simulation."
- Cinema's historical role: to prepare us to live in a world of 2D moving simulations. "Having played this role well, cinema exits the stage. Enters the computer."
