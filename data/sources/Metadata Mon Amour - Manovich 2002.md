# Metadata, Mon Amour

## Summary

An essay on the creative and epistemological potential of image metadata. Manovich argues that the cultural unit has shifted from a single image to a large-scale image database, and that "metadating the image" is not just an industrial problem but a new paradigm for interfacing reality. He identifies four interrelated directions: new structure, new interface, new image, new scale.

## Key Arguments

- **From single image to database**: Comparing *Blow-Up* (1966), *Blade Runner* (1982), and *Minority Report* (2002): the first two search for truth within a single image; the third searches across a database. "By itself, a single image is useless — it only acquires significance in relation to a larger database."
- **Poverty of image description**: Despite two centuries of increasing visual communication, we lack descriptive systems for images comparable to what exists for language (phonetics, syntax, semantics). Stock photography uses a few dozen categories; designers use even fewer ("clean," "futuristic," "corporate"). Barthes's verbocentrism: images cannot signify autonomously without linguistic support.
- **From sampling to complete recording**: Digital storage shifts the paradigm from compressing reality into essential scenes to recording vast continuous chunks — temporal granularity (years of video), visual granularity (360° cameras), social granularity (*The Sims*).
- **Active storage media**: Computers automate searching, sorting, filtering, indexing — "storage media became active." A human no longer needs to watch hundreds of hours of surveillance video.
- **MPEG evolution as cultural shift**: MPEG-1 through MPEG-7 represents gradual shift from low-level metadata (compression settings) to high-level metadata (structure and semantics of media compositions).
- **Four directions**: New structure (MPEG-7, structured media formats) → new interface (navigating media collections) → new image (visual records embedded in virtual space, semantic networks) → new scale (complete recording as creative opportunity).
