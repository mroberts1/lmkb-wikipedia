# Review of Stars Wars: Episode 1

## Summary

A playful, essayistic review of *The Phantom Menace* written from San Diego — military town, former student movement hotbed, now home to Java textbooks. Manovich reads the film as a display of computational resources, a "shameless advertisement for NATO," and the ultimate military parade rendered in pixels. The review combines personal memoir, cultural criticism, and analysis of digital cinema aesthetics.

## Key Arguments

- **Plug-in architecture**: Hollywood films are bricolage structures assembled from market-targeted segments — chase scenes, love subplots, retro references. *Episode 1* follows this pattern but holds together better than most.
- **Digital cathedral**: Special effects films are our civilization's equivalent of Medieval cathedrals — assembled by thousands of skilled craftsmen over years, the ultimate display of collective craftsmanship. But where Medieval masters left stone and glass, we leave pixel sets projected as immaterial light.
- **Computational display**: Strip away humans and plot, and the film is "a pure display of computational resources." Armies of modelers and animators translated into endless rows of vehicles and architectural details. The digital waterfalls obey the "copy and paste" law of digital aesthetics.
- **Corporate campus aesthetics**: The virtual sets look like ultimate corporate campuses — "You can imagine Coruscant housing some future Microsoft / Disney / Getty conglomerate." Waterfalls signify power and wealth in a civilization built in the desert (California).
- **Ultimate student animation**: Many shots resemble textbook 3D animation exercises — "the ultimate student 3D animation of all times," made possible by Lucas's unlimited rendering resources.
- **Military parade**: With its endless armies of Battle Droids, the film is the ultimate military parade, reminiscent of Moscow's Red Square parades — one Empire displaying force for another.
