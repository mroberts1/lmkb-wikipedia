# New Media from Borges to HTML

## Summary

An introduction to the *New Media Reader* anthology that doubles as a comprehensive theoretical overview. Manovich provides a short institutional history of the new media field, then proposes **eight definitions of new media**, arguing that the book's key contribution is revealing the parallel development of modern art and computing after WWII. His boldest claim: computer scientists (Engelbart, Sutherland, Nelson, Berners-Lee) are the most important artists of our time.

## Key Arguments

- **Institutional history**: New media art institutions emerged in Europe/Japan in the late 1980s (ZKM 1989, ISEA 1990). The US art world was the most conservative cultural force, resisting new media throughout the 1990s due to incompatible logics (unique objects vs. copies, gallery distribution vs. network distribution).
- **Software designers as greatest artists**: "The greatest hypertext text is the Web itself… The greatest interactive work is the interactive human-computer interface itself… The greatest avant-garde film is software such as Final Cut Pro."
- **Eight definitions of new media**:
  1. New media vs. cyberculture (cultural/computing vs. social/networking)
  2. Computer technology as distribution platform
  3. Digital data controlled by software (five principles: numerical representation, modularity, automation, variability, transcoding)
  4. Mix between existing cultural conventions and software conventions (image as image-map; camera as data interface)
  5. Aesthetics that accompanies early stages of every new media technology (DV realism = cinéma vérité; QuickTime = Kinetoscope)
  6. Faster execution of algorithms previously executed manually (perspective rendering, combination prints → Photoshop compositing)
  7. Encoding of modernist avant-garde; new media as meta-media (recaps [[Avant-garde as Software - Manovich 1999]])
  8. Parallel articulation in post-WWII art and computing (1960s combinatorics in both minimalism and computer art)
- **Quantity → quality**: Speeding up algorithms doesn't just do the same thing faster — it creates qualitatively new phenomena (perspective rendering → real-time 3D navigation).
