# After Effects, or Velvet Revolution. Part II

## Summary

Part II continues the analysis of the Velvet Revolution, introducing 3D compositing as a new paradigm, the rise of motion graphics (typography and graphic design "imported" into moving images), and the key concept of **deep remixability** — not just mixing media content but the fundamental transformation of media techniques when they are extracted from their original physical medium and turned into algorithms.

## Key Arguments

- **3D compositing**: All media types placed within a single virtual 3D space — 2D sources on planes, 3D models staying three-dimensional, with virtual camera, lights, depth of field, and motion blur. The structure of 3D computer graphics "ate up" lens-based recording as the dominant organizational paradigm.
- **Logic of reversal**: Animation logic (marginal in 20th century) became dominant; 3D graphics (marginal in 1970s-80s) became the umbrella paradigm; typography (marginal to filmmaking) became an equal player. The marginal repeatedly becomes mainstream.
- **From "moving image" to "media composition"**: The concept of a standardized frame sequence no longer captures how designers think. They work on "a modular media composition" — a set of independent objects in 2D or 3D space.
- **Motion graphics take-off**: After Effects on PCs made animated typography affordable to small studios. "The five-hundred-year-old Gutenberg universe came into motion." The whole language of 20th-century graphic design was imported into moving image design.
- **Deep remixability defined**: When media techniques are virtualized (extracted from physical media, turned into algorithms), they start interacting and creating hybrids. Example: depth of field, originally an artifact of lens-based recording, can now be applied to typography in 3D space, animated particles, or any composition — and its parameters can be freely animated. "It became an essentially new technique which functions as a 'character' in its own right."
- **Import/export as foundation**: File compatibility between programs is historically more important than individual operations. The same techniques, compositions, and iconography now appear in any media.
