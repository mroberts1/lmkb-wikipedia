# Generation Flash

## Summary

A multi-part essay identifying a new generation of artists who work in Flash/Shockwave and write their own code, representing a shift from media critique to **software critique** and from commercial media sampling to original code-based creation. Manovich distinguishes three figures — the romantic artist, the media artist (post-modern parasite), and the **software artist** (new romantic) — and analyzes Flash aesthetics through biology, amplification, and the politics of bandwidth.

## Key Arguments

- **Three figures**: (1) Romantic/modernist artist creates from scratch; (2) Media artist (1960s-80s, post-modernism) samples/critiques commercial media — "a parasite who lives at the expense of commercial media"; (3) Software artist (new romantic) writes original code, using modernist abstraction to escape figuration and cinematic language.
- **Flash aesthetics**: Thin lines, low contrast, pixel-thin grids — "Bauhaus design in the service of information design." Biological references (self-regenerating systems, hyper-accelerated evolution) without death (loops run forever). Active amplification of user actions borrowed from computer games.
- **Software critique vs. media critique**: "This generation is no longer interested in 'media critique'… instead it is engaged in software critique." Programming as empowerment; writing code to create cultural systems rather than sampling commercial media.
- **Turntable and remix**: Visual remixing raises questions about how many image tracks can be mixed before the composite becomes noise (answer: about six). Sound allows more layering than vision while maintaining legibility.
- **Flash vs. net art geopolitics**: 1990s net art (simple HTML) included Eastern Europe equally. Flash/Shockwave colonization of the Web restored West/East power structure because it requires fast connections. "The Utopia is over; welcome to the Empire."
- **UTOPIA as political software**: Futurefarmers' UTOPIA "re-writes Marxism as vector graphics" — making connections visible, using data visualization as political tool. "The new rhetoric of interactivity: we get convinced not by listening to a prepared message but by actively working with the data."
