# Image Future

## Summary

A major essay analyzing Universal Capture (U-cap) — the technique developed for *The Matrix* trilogy — as a paradigm for the future of images. Manovich traces the history of 3D computer graphics through two competing paradigms (reality simulation vs. reality sampling) and argues that Universal Capture represents their synthesis, creating a new hybrid image type that is photographic on the surface but structured entirely differently inside.

## Key Arguments

- **Softwarization of moving image**: Software created a common pool of techniques usable across motion graphics, narrative features, animated features, and music videos. Different-looking results are now "deliberate choices rather than the inevitable consequence of differences in production methods."
- **Animation as idea, not medium**: Animation has become a set of general-purpose techniques rather than a separate medium. Most contemporary methods have "animation DNA — mixed with DNA from other media."
- **Reality simulation vs. reality sampling**: Two paradigms coexisted since the 1970s. Simulation builds from scratch via algorithms; sampling captures from existing reality (texture mapping, bump mapping, motion capture). Sampling was long considered "cheating."
- **Universal Capture as synthesis**: Gaeta's method systematically takes physical reality apart and reassembles elements to create a software-based representation. Five synchronized cameras capture actor performance; algorithms track every pixel; results are encoded as 3D animations forming a library. "Neither pure animation, nor cinematography, nor traditional special effects."
- **Uneven development**: The internal "base" of images has been completely replaced by computers, but the visual "superstructure" — photographic appearance — largely remains. Future images will likely still be photographic, "although only on the surface."
- **Hybrid as future**: "Photo-GRAPHIC" — photo providing only an initial layer for the overall graphical mix. The future belongs to hybrids, not pure forms.
