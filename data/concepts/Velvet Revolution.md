# Velvet Revolution

## Definition

Manovich's term for the quiet, largely unnoticed transformation of moving image culture between roughly 1993 and 1998, when compositing and hybrid aesthetics became the default visual language across advertising, music videos, TV graphics, web design, and eventually feature film — without any manifestos, movements, or critical acknowledgment.

## Core Thesis

A revolution in visual language occurred, but it was "velvet" — gradual, non-confrontational, and largely invisible to cultural critics. The revolution consisted of the shift from pure (single-medium) imagery to hybrid imagery created through digital compositing. By ~1998, it was no longer unusual for a single frame to combine photography, 3D graphics, typography, hand-drawn animation, and video — a visual language that would have been impossible, or at least extremely laborious, just five years earlier.

## Key Principles

- **Technology-driven**: The revolution was enabled by specific software tools — primarily After Effects (released 1993), along with Photoshop, Illustrator, and 3D applications — becoming affordable and accessible to individual designers
- **No manifestos**: Unlike earlier visual revolutions (Constructivism, Bauhaus, Swiss Typography), this one had no theoretical program or self-conscious movement. It was driven by practitioners discovering what their tools could do
- **Normalization of hybridity**: What began as experimental quickly became the default. By the 2000s, pure unmanipulated footage in commercial media became the exception rather than the rule
- **Below critical radar**: Film and media studies continued to discuss narrative, representation, and ideology while the visual language itself was undergoing its most dramatic transformation since the introduction of sound
