# New Media Art

## Definition

Art that uses digital technologies as its medium — net art, software art, interactive installations, generative art, virtual reality art. Manovich has a complex relationship with this field: he helped define its theoretical foundations while consistently arguing that it occupies a problematic position between the art world and the technology world.

## Core Thesis

New media art exists in an institutional gap: too technological for the contemporary art world, too artistic for the tech industry. Manovich argues this marginality is partly the field's own fault — new media art too often privileges interactivity and technology over aesthetic quality, and relies on the "Turing test" (can the simulation fool you?) rather than developing genuinely new aesthetic strategies. At the same time, the contemporary art world's refusal to engage with digital tools is equally provincial.

## Key Arguments

- **Two art worlds**: New media art and contemporary art operate as separate institutional systems with different venues, critics, histories, and values. Neither adequately engages with the other
- **Against the Turing test**: Too much new media art measures success by technical achievement (how realistic? how interactive?) rather than aesthetic or conceptual innovation
- **Ten key texts**: In [[10 Key Texts on New Media Art - Manovich 2002]], Manovich identifies foundational texts that should inform new media art practice — from Greenberg's formalism to Bourdieu's field theory to Whitelaw's metacreation
- **Generation Flash**: In [[Generation Flash - Manovich 2002]], Manovich champions artists who write their own software (Flash, Processing) as representing the most productive direction — creating rather than merely selecting, building tools rather than just using them
- **Don't call it art**: In [[Dont Call it Art - Manovich 2003]], reviewing Ars Electronica 2003, Manovich argues that the best new media work may not fit conventional "art" categories at all — it might be better understood as design, engineering, or a new cultural practice altogether
- **Post-Web 2.0 challenge**: In [[Practice of Everyday Media Life - Manovich 2008]], Manovich argues the real challenge to professional art is not amateur quality but the *dynamics* of Web 2.0 culture — its energy, innovation, and unpredictability
