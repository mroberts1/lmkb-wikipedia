# Automation of Vision

## Definition

The delegation of perceptual and representational tasks — seeing, recognizing, mapping, tracking — from human operators to computational systems. Manovich traces this from military imaging technologies through computer vision to digital photography, arguing that automation fundamentally transforms the cultural meaning of visual representation.

## Core Thesis

The history of visual technology is a progressive automation of vision: from perspectival drawing aids (camera obscura) to photography (automating image capture) to digital imaging (automating image analysis, enhancement, and generation). Each stage removes human agency from a different part of the visual process. Digital imaging completes the circle — images can now be created, processed, and interpreted entirely by machines.

## Key Principles

- **Military origins**: Key imaging technologies (radar, infrared, computer vision, real-time displays) were developed for military purposes before becoming cultural tools. The genealogy of the interface includes military vision systems
- **Photography's paradox**: Photography automated capture but retained the human eye's perspective. Digital photography breaks this — images can be computationally generated, enhanced, and analyzed without human perception at any stage
- **From representation to data**: Automated vision treats images as data to be processed, not representations to be interpreted. This shifts the image from a cultural/aesthetic object to a computational resource
- **Synthetic realism**: Computer graphics aims to automate not just capture but the entire visual pipeline — modeling, lighting, rendering — producing images that look photographically "real" without any photographic process

## Key Sources

### Engineering and automation (1994-1997)
In [[Engineering of Vision - Manovich 1994]], Manovich analyzes how digital technologies "engineer" visual perception. In [[Automation of Sight - Manovich 1997]], he examines computer vision, satellite imaging, and medical imaging as instances where seeing is delegated to machines.

### Photography and indexicality (1994-1998)
In [[Paradoxes of Digital Photography - Manovich 1994]], Manovich challenges the assumption that digital photography is simply "better photography" — digitization transforms the image's ontological status. In [[The Camera and the World - Manovich 1998]], he analyzes how virtual cameras in 3D graphics simulate but fundamentally alter photographic vision.

### Military genealogy (1995-1997)
In [[Genealogy of the Interface - Manovich 1997]], Manovich traces how military real-time displays (radar screens, heads-up displays) became the templates for civilian computer interfaces.
