# Remix Culture

## Definition

The broad cultural condition in which existing cultural material is systematically recombined, reworked, and redistributed as a primary mode of cultural production. Manovich analyzes remix across music, video, web content, fashion, design, and software — distinguishing it from related concepts like appropriation, quotation, and collage.

## Core Thesis

Remix has moved from a specialized music production technique (Tom Moulton's disco remixes, 1972) to a general cultural logic. On the web, remix has become an *epistemology* — knowledge is generated by selecting and combining from multiple sources (RSS feeds, Google News, blog commentary). Manovich distinguishes surface-level content remix (mashups, anime music videos, blog reposting) from [[Deep Remixability]] — the software-enabled remixing of techniques and working methods from different media.

## Key Principles

- **Remixing ≠ appropriation**: Appropriation (Duchamp, Warhol, Prince) transfers a sign from one sphere to another. Remixing systematically reworks the original — it's a more engaged, productive operation
- **Sampling ≠ quoting**: Quoting inserts fragments into a new text. Sampling is industrialized collage/montage — mechanical reproduction applied to fragments rather than whole works
- **Three differences from modernist collage**: (1) Samples arranged in loops, not linear sequence; (2) Sound allows blending into coherent whole, not just clash; (3) Electronic musicians expect their work to be remixed
- **Remix epistemology**: On the web, knowledge production itself becomes remixing — bloggers repost with commentary, news aggregators algorithmically recombine sources, Wikipedia assembles knowledge from multiple contributors
- **Selection as creation**: When culture is a vast database, authoring means selecting and combining — the DJ, the web surfer, and the Flash programmer all create through selection from pre-existing elements
- **Modularity enables remix**: Post-computer modularity ("content wants to be granular") — RSS feeds, APIs, web services — makes cultural content inherently remixable

## Evolution of the Concept

### Authorship and selection (2002)
In [[Who is the Author - Manovich 2002]], Manovich explores how digital tools redistribute authorship across multiple agents — human and software. Selection from menus and presets becomes a creative act.

### Modularity and collaborative remixability (2005)
In [[Remixability and Modularity - Manovich 2005]], Manovich analyzes how modular content design enables collaborative remixability — Wikipedia, open-source software, and RSS feeds as remix-native forms.

### Remix survey (2007)
In [[What Comes After Remix - Manovich 2007]], Manovich provides the comprehensive survey: tracing remix from Moulton through Coldcut, across music/video/web/fashion, and asking what cultural form might succeed remix.

### Strategies and tactics (2008)
In [[Practice of Everyday Media Life - Manovich 2008]], Manovich uses de Certeau's framework to analyze how corporate strategies now incorporate remix logic (customization, hackability) while user tactics (blogs, AMVs) become visible and permanent.
