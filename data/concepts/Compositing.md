# Compositing

## Definition

The assembly of multiple visual elements — live-action footage, CGI, 2D graphics, typography, effects — into a single integrated image or sequence. For Manovich, compositing is the key operation of digital visual culture, replacing montage as the dominant principle of visual construction.

## Core Thesis

Where 20th-century visual culture was defined by montage (the juxtaposition of different images in sequence), 21st-century visual culture is defined by compositing (the seamless integration of different elements within a single image). Compositing creates spatial depth and hybrid imagery rather than temporal contrast. The shift from montage to compositing represents a fundamental change in how visual meaning is constructed.

## Key Principles

- **Montage → compositing**: Montage creates meaning through collision between shots; compositing creates meaning through layering within a single image. Montage foregrounds discontinuity; compositing typically aims for seamless continuity
- **Layers and transparency**: The layer-based paradigm (pioneered in Photoshop, extended in After Effects) is the structural principle. Each layer has adjustable transparency (alpha channel), enabling complex combinations
- **Two aesthetics**: (1) Seamless compositing that hides the joins (Hollywood VFX) vs. (2) compositing that reveals its own layered construction (motion graphics, art)
- **Time-based → composition-based**: After Effects shifted moving image production from editing (assembling shots in time) to compositing (assembling elements in space within each frame). The timeline still exists but serves spatial composition rather than narrative editing

## Evolution of the Concept

### Theoretical foundation (1995-1997)
In [[What is Digital Cinema - Manovich 1995]], compositing is identified as the operation that makes digital cinema possible — live action becomes just one layer among many. In [[Behind the Screen - Manovich 1997]], the physical history of layered screens is traced.

### After Effects analysis (2006)
In [[After Effects Part I - Manovich 2006]], Manovich provides the most detailed analysis: After Effects' layer-based interface enabled the [[Velvet Revolution]] by making compositing accessible to individual designers. The shift from editing suites (Avid) to compositing tools (After Effects) reorganized visual production.

### Deep remixability (2006-2007)
In [[After Effects Part II - Manovich 2006]] and [[Understanding Hybrid Media - Manovich 2007]], compositing is revealed as the technical enabler of [[Deep Remixability]] — it's through compositing that techniques from different media actually combine into new hybrids.
