# Hybrid Media

## Definition

Manovich's term for the genuinely new media forms that emerge when techniques from previously separate media are combined through software. Hybrid media are distinct from *multimedia* (media placed side by side) — in hybrids, the DNA of different media fuses to create new species that could not exist in any single prior medium.

## Core Thesis

Starting in the late 1990s, an "invisible revolution" occurred: media started combining at the level of techniques, not just content. By ~1998, hybrid moving images (combining photography, cinema, animation, painting, typography, 3D graphics) became the norm across advertising, music videos, TV design, and web interfaces. This revolution went unnoticed by cultural critics because no individual new medium was invented — the novelty was in the combinations.

## Key Principles

- **Hybrid ≠ multimedia**: Multimedia places media side by side (text next to image next to video). Hybrid media fuse techniques from different media into a single new form where the components are inseparable
- **Three types of hybridity** (from Korean Edition introduction): (1) Different media combined in a single object; (2) New "species" of media objects; (3) Hybrid aesthetics combining strategies from distinct traditions (e.g., modernist minimalism + complexity theory)
- **Continuity turn**: Both temporal forms (motion graphics) and spatial forms (architecture) shifted toward continuous change and transformation. Discrete cuts gave way to morphs; angular geometry gave way to smooth curves
- **Compatible but distinct**: Different media can combine while preserving their identifiable properties — a viewer can recognize the photographic layer, the typographic layer, the 3D layer, even as they are fused

## Key Examples

- **Jeremy Blake**: Paintings that continuously layer, dissolve, and fade — hybrid of painting, photography, and cinema
- **Takeshi Murata**: Video that permanently metamorphoses between live-action and abstract digital — hybrid of photography and algorithmic generation
- **Yokohama Port Terminal** (FOA): Architecture generated through animation software, producing smooth continuous forms — hybrid of architectural modeling and cinematic animation tools
- **Motion graphics**: The entire field as paradigmatic hybrid form

## Evolution of the Concept

### Early observations (1999-2003)
In [[Review of Stars Wars Episode 1 - Manovich 1999]], Manovich observes the composite nature of digital cinema. In [[Introduction to Korean Edition - Manovich 2003]], he outlines three types of hybridity and a "third aesthetics" that is neither modernist reduction nor postmodernist quotation.

### Compositing analysis (2006)
In [[After Effects Part I - Manovich 2006]] and [[After Effects Part II - Manovich 2006]], Manovich shows how compositing software creates hybrid images in practice. The [[Velvet Revolution]] is the historical process by which hybridity became the default visual language.

### Image future (2006)
In [[Image Future - Manovich 2006]], Manovich analyzes the hybrid image through the Matrix trilogy's Universal Capture technology — images that are simultaneously captured and constructed, photographic and synthetic.

### Full synthesis (2007)
In [[Understanding Hybrid Media - Manovich 2007]], the concept reaches its mature form with the "continuity turn" thesis and detailed analysis of Blake, Murata, and Lislegaard as three distinct articulations of hybrid aesthetics.
