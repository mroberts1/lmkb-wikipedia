# Interactivity

## Definition

The capacity of a media object to respond to user input — clicking, navigating, choosing, manipulating. While typically celebrated as empowering, Manovich offers a sustained critique: interactivity can be ideologically manipulative, aesthetically impoverishing, and conceptually confused.

## Core Thesis

Manovich argues that "interactivity" is a myth in most implementations. Clicking through predetermined paths in a hypertext or choosing from a menu of options is not genuine agency — it's following someone else's script while being given the illusion of choice. He connects this to broader political structures: interactivity can function as a technology of control, directing users through prescribed pathways while creating the feeling of freedom.

## Key Arguments

- **Totalitarian interactivity**: In [[On Totalitarian Interactivity - Manovich 1996]], Manovich draws a provocative parallel between interactive media and totalitarian political systems — both offer structured choices within a framework that determines all possible outcomes. The user/citizen feels free while following a predetermined script
- **Externalization of mental processes**: In [[Externalization of Psyche - Manovich 1995]], Manovich argues that interactive media externalize the viewer's psychological processes (association, memory, imagination) into explicit interface structures (links, menus, maps). What was internal and unique becomes external and standardized
- **Branching vs. procedural**: Simple branching structures (choose your own adventure) offer only the illusion of choice. Procedural, algorithmic interactivity (games, simulations) is more genuinely open-ended
- **Against interactivity as progress**: The assumption that interactivity is always better than passive reception is challenged — a great film or novel may produce richer mental engagement than a mediocre interactive experience
