# Cultural Analytics

## Definition

The use of computational methods — data visualization, pattern recognition, statistics, machine learning — to analyze large collections of cultural artifacts (images, films, designs, user-generated media). Manovich founded the Cultural Analytics Lab (2007) and coined the term to describe this approach, which bridges the humanities and computer science.

## Core Thesis

Traditional cultural analysis examines individual works or small samples. Digital culture produces millions of artifacts daily — Flickr photos, YouTube videos, Instagram posts, DeviantArt designs. Understanding culture at this scale requires computational tools: visualization to reveal patterns invisible to close reading, and quantitative methods to identify trends across vast datasets. Cultural analytics does not replace close reading but adds a "distant reading" (Moretti) dimension.

## Key Principles

- **Scale as epistemology**: Analyzing millions of images reveals patterns invisible when studying individual works. Scale is not just quantity but a qualitative shift in what can be known about culture
- **Visualization as analysis**: Plotting cultural artifacts along multiple dimensions (color, composition, date, location) produces visual patterns that constitute a new form of cultural knowledge
- **Beyond text**: While digital humanities initially focused on text (topic modeling, sentiment analysis), cultural analytics emphasizes visual and multimedia objects — images, video, design, interfaces
- **Democratic scope**: Cultural analytics can study user-generated content and everyday media alongside canonical art — mapping the "long tail" of cultural production

## Evolution of the Concept

### Precursors: data art and visualization (2002-2005)
In [[Data Visualization as New Abstraction - Manovich 2002]], Manovich analyzes data visualization art (mapping data to visual forms) and critiques the "anti-sublime" — the desire to make vast datasets comprehensible. In [[The Shape of Information - Manovich 2005]], he proposes info-aesthetics as a field connecting information visualization to aesthetic theory.

### Social data and group portraits (2006)
In [[Social Data Browsing - Manovich 2006]], Manovich analyzes Golan Levin's *The Dumpster* as a "group portrait for the data mining age" — visualizing 20,000 blog posts about romantic breakups. This prefigures cultural analytics' approach to user-generated content.

### Scale effects (2005)
In [[Scale Effects - Manovich 2005]], Manovich argues that scaling up changes qualitative experience — 4K resolution creates different aesthetic possibilities than SD, and large-scale cultural databases enable different kinds of analysis than single-artwork study.

### Web 2.0 and mass production (2008)
In [[Practice of Everyday Media Life - Manovich 2008]], the explosion of user-generated content creates the dataset that cultural analytics is designed to study — millions of creative artifacts that can be computationally mapped and analyzed.
