# Software Studies

## Definition

Manovich's proposed field of study that treats software as a cultural force — not merely a tool but the engine that shapes how we create, access, and understand culture. Just as the printing press or cinema reshaped culture in their eras, software is the invisible layer that now mediates virtually all cultural production and consumption.

## Core Thesis

Software has become "the universal engine of contemporary culture," yet cultural theory largely ignores it. Media studies focuses on content; computer science focuses on algorithms. Software studies bridges this gap by analyzing how specific software tools (Photoshop, After Effects, Flash, Google) shape cultural forms, aesthetics, and practices. "We live in a software culture — that is, a culture where the production, distribution, and reception of most content is mediated by software."

## Key Principles

- **Software as cultural layer**: Software doesn't just deliver culture — it actively produces it through its interface metaphors, default operations, and built-in assumptions
- **Filters, plug-ins, and menus**: The specific tools available in software (Photoshop filters, After Effects plug-ins) define the universe of possible cultural operations. What is easy to do in software becomes culturally dominant
- **Avant-garde as software**: The radical techniques of 20th-century avant-garde art (montage, collage, defamiliarization) have been literally implemented as software operations available to all users — democratized but also routinized
- **Permanent extendibility**: Unlike physical tools, software can be endlessly extended through plug-ins, scripts, and new modules — making it a fundamentally open-ended cultural medium

## Evolution of the Concept

### Precursors (1997-1998)
In [[Filters Plug-ins and Menus - Manovich 1998]], Manovich begins analyzing how Photoshop's specific tool vocabulary shapes visual culture. The filter becomes an aesthetic operator, and the menu becomes a cultural taxonomy.

### Foundational statement (1999)
In [[Avant-garde as Software - Manovich 1999]], Manovich makes the key argument: the techniques of Eisenstein, Vertov, Moholy-Nagy, and other avant-gardists are now literally implemented in consumer software. The avant-garde's greatest achievement was not individual artworks but new ways of seeing — which now exist as software commands.

### Alan Kay and the metamedium (2006)
In [[Alan Kays Universal Media Machine - Manovich 2006]], Manovich roots software studies historically in Kay and Goldberg's 1977 concept of the computer as a "metamedium" — a medium that simulates all other media and adds new properties impossible in any physical medium.

### After Effects and cultural production (2006)
In [[After Effects Part I - Manovich 2006]] and [[After Effects Part II - Manovich 2006]], Manovich demonstrates software studies in practice — analyzing how After Effects' specific interface (layers, timeline, compositing) enabled a "velvet revolution" in moving image culture.

### Import/export and compatibility (2006)
In [[Import Export - Manovich 2006]], Manovich shows how software's ability to import/export between formats creates the conditions for [[Hybrid Media]] — interoperability between programs is the technical foundation of deep remixability.
