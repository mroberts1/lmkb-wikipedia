# Digital Cinema

## Definition

For Manovich, digital cinema represents a return to the pre-cinematic practices of manual construction, animation, and painting — with live-action footage becoming just one input among many. Rather than being a simple upgrade of analog film, digitization fundamentally redefines what cinema is.

## Core Thesis

Manovich's foundational argument (1995): "Digital cinema is a particular case of animation that uses live-action footage as one of its many elements." Once footage is digitized, it loses its indexical privilege — it becomes paintable, compositable, and manipulable like any other graphic material. Cinema thus returns to its 19th-century origins in animation (Reynaud, Méliès) rather than continuing the Lumière tradition of photographic recording.

## Key Principles

- **Live action as raw material**: Film footage becomes data to be algorithmically processed, not a transparent window onto reality
- **From kino-eye to kino-brush**: The camera's automatic recording gives way to manual/algorithmic construction frame by frame
- **Five principles of digital cinema**: (1) live action as raw material for compositing; (2) once digitized, footage is indistinguishable from animation; (3) live-action footage is only one element; (4) digital cinema = painting + cinema + image processing + compositing + 2D animation + 3D animation; (5) digital cinema is a subgenre of painting
- **Elastic reality**: All spatial and temporal parameters become continuously variable — what was fixed in analog (frame rate, camera position, lighting) becomes editable

## Evolution of the Concept

### Early formulation (1995)
In [[What is Digital Cinema - Manovich 1995]], Manovich establishes the core thesis by analyzing Jurassic Park, Forrest Gump, and historical precedents. Cinema's identity as "photographic recording" was a historical accident; digital tools restore the broader possibilities.

### DV and realism (2001)
In [[DV Realism to Universal Recording Machine - Manovich 2001]], Manovich identifies two responses to digital video: (1) dogma-style "DV realism" emphasizing handheld rawness, and (2) using DV as a "universal recording machine" that captures any visual situation. Lars von Trier and Mike Figgis represent these poles.

### Compositing revolution (2003-2006)
In [[Moving Image after Computerization - Manovich 2003]] and the After Effects essays ([[After Effects Part I - Manovich 2006]], [[After Effects Part II - Manovich 2006]]), the focus shifts from feature film to the broader moving image ecology. After Effects' layer-based compositing enables a "velvet revolution" where hybrid moving images become the norm across advertising, music videos, TV, and web.

### Hybrid images (2006-2007)
In [[Image Future - Manovich 2006]] and [[Understanding Hybrid Media - Manovich 2007]], Manovich analyzes how digital compositing creates genuinely new image types — neither photography nor painting nor animation, but hybrids that combine techniques from all previously separate media.
