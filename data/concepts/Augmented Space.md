# Augmented Space

## Definition

Physical space overlaid with dynamically changing multimedia information — data displays, projections, wireless networks, surveillance feeds. Manovich proposes augmented space as the conceptual successor to virtual reality: rather than creating entirely synthetic environments, augmented space layers data onto the physical world.

## Core Thesis

Every point in physical space is increasingly addressable — identifiable and targetable with information. This transforms architecture from the design of physical structures to the design of information flows through space. Manovich argues that architects, artists, and designers should think of physical space as a "data space" — and that the history of this practice stretches back through murals, frescoes, and Baroque ceiling paintings, not just digital technology.

## Key Principles

- **From VR to augmented space**: The 1990s focused on replacing physical space with virtual space. The 2000s shift to layering data onto physical space — GPS, wireless networks, electronic displays, cell-based addressing
- **Every point is addressable**: Cell-space technology (wireless networks, GPS, RFID) means every location can receive and transmit data. Physical space becomes a display surface and data channel
- **Architecture as information design**: The architect's role expands from designing physical form to designing how information inhabits and flows through space
- **Historical genealogy**: Augmented space has deep precedents — frescoed churches, illuminated manuscripts, department store displays. Digital technology extends a centuries-old practice of overlaying information onto physical space
- **Surveillance as augmentation**: Video surveillance is the dark twin of augmented space — both involve layering data onto physical locations, but surveillance extracts data rather than delivering it
