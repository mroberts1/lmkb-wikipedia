# Deep Remixability

## Definition

Manovich's term for the distinctively new kind of remixing enabled by software: not just remixing *content* from different media (as in a music mashup or anime music video), but remixing the fundamental *techniques, working methods, and ways of representation* from previously separate media. Deep remixability produces genuinely new hybrid forms that could not exist in any single prior medium.

## Core Thesis

When media are simulated in software, their techniques become separable from their original physical substrates. Typography techniques can be applied to video, cinematic camera movements to 3D graphics, painterly effects to photographs — all within the same software environment. The result is not multimedia (media side by side) but hybrid media: new species that combine DNA from multiple parents.

## Key Principles

- **Content remix vs. technique remix**: Content remix (sampling, mashups, collage) recombines existing media objects. Deep remixability recombines the *operations* and *techniques* of different media — a fundamentally different and more generative process
- **Techniques become algorithms**: Computerization extracts media techniques from their physical substrates and turns them into software algorithms. These algorithms can then be applied to any media type, not just the original one
- **Supercharged techniques**: Once virtualized, techniques gain extended range, explicit controls, and programmability that were impossible in their physical form
- **The variable form**: Software represents all phenomena as sets of continuously variable parameters. What was fixed becomes variable; what was constant becomes animatable. This "aesthetics of continuity" defines contemporary visual culture

## Evolution of the Concept

### Precursors: modularity and remixability (2005)
In [[Remixability and Modularity - Manovich 2005]], Manovich identifies how post-computer modularity enables collaborative remixability — content designed to be granular and recombined. This sets the stage for the deeper insight about technique remixing.

### First formulation (2006)
In [[After Effects Part II - Manovich 2006]], Manovich coins "deep remixability" while analyzing motion graphics and After Effects workflows. He shows how a single composition can combine hand-drawn animation, photography, cinematography, 3D graphics, typography, and painting techniques — producing a "new species" of moving image.

### Full synthesis (2007)
In [[Understanding Hybrid Media - Manovich 2007]], the concept is fully elaborated through analysis of artists Jeremy Blake, Takeshi Murata, and Ann Lislegaard. Manovich connects deep remixability to the broader "continuity turn" in visual culture and architecture.

### Remix culture context (2007)
In [[What Comes After Remix - Manovich 2007]], Manovich surveys remix culture broadly — music remixes, blogs, anime music videos — to distinguish surface-level content remixing from the deeper technique remixing that is unique to software-based production.
